Terms of Service; Didn't Read Does consent work?


This is a rough draft that I wrote to practice for my talk:

1 Terms of Service; Didn't Read

My name is Hugo. I have been working on “Terms of Service; Didn't Read” since June. We're a not-for-profit project started by three Europeans.

2 Our goal is to fix what we call the biggest lie

on the web. This panel is about consent. We say here that “consent works.” But what does that mean exactly? I would like to challenge that assumption because I believe this is one core aspect of the problem of personal data: consent does not really work.

What we need is clarity.

3 The state of consent on the web

This is the sad reality of what constitutes “consent” today. Users are usually left out in legal obscurity, sometimes just a small link. Then users just check a box or click a button saying that they have read and agree to the terms.

However this is most of the time a big lie.

MEP Corazza Bildt said earlier that, I quote, “people are not victims” and people are “ready to trade off in order to use Facebook.” But we don't actually know if people are ready to do that, because people are not properly informed about their rights online.

4 76 days

This is from a US research. It would take each person 76 days every year to really read the terms. This is not possible.

5 the terms of service of iTunes are longer than Hamlet

You basically have to be a full time lawyer in order to understand how your rights are being bargained online.

6 What can we do?

Amendment 118 to implement an icon system. I welcome this proposal.

In the field of copyright, the Creative Commons initiative has already done a lot to ease the use and sharing of copyrighted content lawfully.

7 but terms of service and privacy policies are very diverse

and they can be complicated. So an private initiative like Creative Commons is likely to encounter problems of scale here. Imagine a set of 160 icons, the project becomes effectiveless.

8 Terms of Service Didn't Read

We thought that there was a need to display a summary of terms, to get the most important information instantly.

9 An example with Facebook

A summary of the copyright license. This is something that we could make mandatory, an innovative kind of formalism in order to protect users. We can identify some rights that are important:

  • copyright

  • privacy rights

    especially regarding third party's access to personal data and user device information

  • rights to a trial, etc.

10 One big problem: consent on changes

Terms of service, as you know, change all the time. It is impossible for one person to keep track of them. Your consent is worth nothing if the text you agree to has changed without you noticing. Strong obligations of information should be enforced on substantial changes.

BTW today in Brazil, we are also organising an event where hackers join forces to develop a new tool that will allow us to keep track of all changes in all terms.

11 Icons

This is a screenshot of the projects for Privacy Icons. Privacy Icons and ToS;DR make a good fit together and we plan to show you how the consensual process can be improved for more informed decisions about citizens' rights.

What is really important is not only the summary, but the summary must also give pointers and advice to the user, so that users are able to dive in the specific aspects of terms that they might care about.

This is what explicit consent looks like.

12 Thank you

As a conclusion, I would like to emphasise that

  • the consent process needs to change, it is currently broken and leaves no real choice to the users because citizens are not aware of how their rights online are being managed by web services
  • a set of important questions regarding opt-ins must be defined to protect fundamental rights, privacy and authors' rights
  • clarity and formalism can help but they're not enough.

The basic problem with web services is that the user is not in control of the software. Web service providers are in control. There is a systematic problem of power balance at the expense of citizens rights, that we need to restore; for instance with the possibility of collective enforcement.
